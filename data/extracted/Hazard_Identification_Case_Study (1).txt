HAZARD IDENTIFICATION IN 
INDUSTRIAL WORKPLACES 
Introduction to Hazard Identification 
Hazard identification is the systematic process of recognizing potential sources of 
harm in a workplace environment. It forms the first and most critical step in the 
overall risk management framework adopted by industries worldwide. Without proper 
hazard identification, organizations cannot take appropriate preventive or corrective 
actions. 
In industrial settings, hazards can arise from machinery, chemicals, work processes, 
environmental conditions, or human behavior. Identifying these hazards early allows 
safety officers, engineers, and management to put in place timely control measures 
that protect workers and organizational assets. 
‚úì‚Äã Hazard identification is a legal requirement under most national occupational 
health and safety regulations. 
‚úì‚Äã It is a proactive approach that minimizes reliance on reactive accident 
investigation. 
‚úì‚Äã Effective identification reduces insurance costs, downtime, and liability 
exposure. 
 
Importance of Workplace Safety 
Workplace safety is not merely a regulatory obligation; it is a moral and operational 
necessity. Industrial injuries and fatalities result in human suffering, loss of 
productivity, legal consequences, and reputational damage. A culture of safety 
benefits both employers and employees. 
‚óè‚Äã Worker health and well-being are protected when hazards are proactively 
managed. 
‚óè‚Äã Organizations that invest in safety see higher employee morale and 
productivity. 
‚óè‚Äã Legal compliance with standards such as OSHA 29 CFR 1910 is maintained. 
‚óè‚Äã Financial losses from accidents, compensation claims, and plant shutdowns 
are reduced.

Industry Overview  
The selected case study is based on a medium-scale chemical manufacturing plant 
located in Pune, Maharashtra, India. The plant, referred to as "ChemiCore 
Industries," was established in 2005 and operates across an area of approximately 
12 acres. It employs around 480 workers across three production shifts. 
ChemiCore Industries manufactures industrial-grade solvents, adhesives, and 
coating compounds used primarily in the automotive and construction sectors. The 
facility includes reactor units, storage tanks, a quality control laboratory, packaging 
units, and a waste treatment plant.

‚óã‚Äã Location: Pune Industrial Zone, Maharashtra, India 
‚óã‚Äã Workforce: 480 direct employees, 80 contract workers 
‚óã‚Äã Operating Hours: 24 hours, 3 shifts per day 
‚óã‚Äã Regulatory Body: Maharashtra Pollution Control Board (MPCB), Factory Act

Work Environment Description 
The production floor at ChemiCore Industries is characterised by high ambient 
temperatures, chemical vapors, and continuous mechanical noise. Workers are 

 
exposed to rotating equipment, pressurised pipelines, flammable liquid transfers, and 
elevated work platforms. 
a.‚Äã Reactor bay temperatures often exceed 45 degrees Celsius during peak 
operations. 
b.‚Äã Chemical fumes from solvent storage units are a persistent concern. 
c.‚Äã Noise levels in the mixing and filling areas frequently cross 90 dB. 
d.‚Äã Many work areas require working at heights above 3 meters. 
Introduction to the Case Scenario 
On the morning of 14th February 2022, a serious chemical spillage and fire incident 
occurred in Reactor Bay No. 3 at ChemiCore Industries. The incident resulted in two 
workers sustaining severe chemical burns, one case of acute inhalation injury, and 
significant damage to equipment worth approximately INR 35 lakhs. The plant was 
partially shut down for six days. 
The incident triggered a full-scale internal investigation as well as an inspection by 
the District Factory Inspectorate. The findings revealed multiple pre-existing hazards 
that had not been properly identified or controlled, pointing to critical gaps in the 
plant's hazard identification program. 
 
Company Profile and Operations 
ChemiCore Industries operates under ISO 9001:2015 for quality management and 
had attempted to implement elements of ISO 45001:2018 for occupational health 
and safety, though full certification had not yet been achieved at the time of the 
incident. 
‚óè‚Äã Product Lines: Industrial solvents (toluene-based), epoxy adhesives, 
polyurethane coatings 
‚óè‚Äã Annual Turnover: Approximately INR 120 crores 
‚óè‚Äã Safety Officer: One certified safety officer per shift (DISH-certified) 
‚óè‚Äã Last Safety Audit: Conducted 8 months before the incident 
‚óè‚Äã Accident Record (prior 3 years): 4 minor incidents, 1 lost-time injury 
 
Nature of Work Activities 
The plant performs a range of high-risk industrial activities, many of which involve 
the handling of hazardous substances. Workers perform tasks in environments 
where multiple hazard types coexist simultaneously. 
1.‚Äã Mixing and blending of flammable organic solvents in open-top reactor 
vessels. 
2.‚Äã Transfer of raw materials from storage tankers to process units via hose 
connections. 
3.‚Äã Quality inspection of products using spectroscopic instruments in enclosed 
labs. 

 
4.‚Äã Maintenance of equipment including welding, grinding, and electrical work 
near flammable areas. 
5.‚Äã Loading and unloading of finished goods using forklifts in congested 
warehouse areas. 
Each of these activities presents its own set of hazards that require careful 
identification and documentation before work begins. Pre-task hazard assessments 
had not been consistently performed across all these areas. 
Description of the Incident 
On the day of the incident, Reactor Bay No. 3 was scheduled to complete a batch of 
toluene-based adhesive compound. The shift began at 6:00 AM with a team of five 
workers, including one process operator, two helpers, and one maintenance 
technician who had been called in to attend to a leaking valve on the reactor feed 
line. 
At approximately 9:15 AM, while the maintenance technician attempted to tighten the 
leaking valve without isolating the feed line, a sudden surge in line pressure caused 
the valve fitting to fail. Approximately 40 litres of toluene were released onto the 
reactor bay floor. The vapors rapidly spread and were ignited by a nearby electric 
motor that had not been placed in an explosion-proof enclosure. 
The resulting flash fire injured two workers directly and caused damage to 
instrumentation panels and pipework in the bay. Emergency response was initiated 
within three minutes, and the fire was suppressed within fifteen minutes using dry 
chemical powder extinguishers. 
 
 
Sequence of Events Leading to Hazard 
The following sequence outlines the chain of failures that led to the incident: 

 
a.‚Äã Valve leak on feed line was reported but not formally logged in the 
maintenance register. 
b.‚Äã Maintenance work order was issued verbally without a Permit to Work (PTW) 
being raised. 
c.‚Äã The technician entered the bay without isolating the feed line or conducting 
lockout/tagout (LOTO). 
d.‚Äã No gas detection equipment was deployed in the bay prior to the maintenance 
activity. 
e.‚Äã Electric motor near the work area had not been inspected for explosion-proof 
compliance. 
f.‚Äã Workers did not receive a pre-task safety briefing that morning. 
Identification of Hazards in the Case 
Following the incident, a multi-disciplinary investigation team comprising the safety 
officer, a process engineer, a human resources representative, and an external 
safety consultant conducted a comprehensive hazard identification exercise across 
Reactor Bay No. 3 and adjoining areas. The following hazards were formally 
identified and recorded: 
‚úì‚Äã Presence of highly flammable toluene vapors with no continuous gas 
monitoring system. 
‚úì‚Äã Non-explosion-proof electrical motor operating in a classified hazardous area. 
‚úì‚Äã Degraded valve fitting on the reactor feed line showing prior signs of 
corrosion. 
‚úì‚Äã Inadequate lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedure for maintenance isolation. 
‚úì‚Äã No formal Permit to Work (PTW) system in place for high-risk maintenance 
tasks. 
‚úì‚Äã Poor housekeeping with accumulated chemical residue on the bay floor. 
‚úì‚Äã Inadequate personal protective equipment (PPE) usage among workers on 
shift. 
Sources of Hazards 
Hazards in this case emerged from multiple sources simultaneously, indicating a 
systemic breakdown rather than a single point of failure. 
‚óè‚Äã Process Source: Handling and transfer of toluene, a Class IB flammable liquid 
(flash point 4 degrees Celsius), without adequate containment controls. 
‚óè‚Äã Equipment Source: Ageing valve fittings and non-compliant electrical 
equipment in classified areas. 
‚óè‚Äã Procedural Source: Absence of a formal PTW system and pre-task hazard 
assessment. 
‚óè‚Äã Human Source: Lack of safety training, bypassing of established isolation 
procedures, and poor communication between shifts. 
‚óè‚Äã Environmental Source: Enclosed reactor bay with insufficient ventilation and 
no forced air extraction. 
Unsafe Acts and Conditions 

 
The investigation distinguished between  
1.‚Äã unsafe acts (human behaviors) 
2.‚Äã unsafe conditions (physical/environmental deficiencies) 
Unsafe Acts: 
‚óã‚Äã Performing maintenance work without a valid Permit to Work. 
‚óã‚Äã Failing to perform lockout/tagout before working on pressurized systems. 
‚óã‚Äã Entering a confined area with potential vapor accumulation without gas

Unsafe Conditions: 
‚óã‚Äã Defective and corroded valve installed on a flammable liquid feed line.

‚óã‚Äã No mechanical ventilation system in Reactor Bay No. 3. 
‚óã‚Äã Absence of permanent fixed gas detectors in the reactor bay. 
Types of Hazards Involved 
The investigation identified hazards spanning multiple categories, highlighting the 
complexity of the safety failure: 
1.‚Äã Chemical Hazards: Toluene vapors posing inhalation toxicity and 
fire/explosion risk. Toluene has a TWA-TLV of 50 ppm as per ACGIH 
guidelines. 
2.‚Äã Physical Hazards: High ambient temperature, excessive noise from reactor 
agitators, and pressurized pipelines. 
3.‚Äã Electrical Hazards: Non-explosion-proof motor in a Zone 1 classified 
hazardous area, posing ignition risk. 
4.‚Äã Biological Hazards: Absence of permit systems, inadequate training, and poor 
communication channels between shifts and departments. 
 

 
Most Critical Hazard Identified 
The most critical hazard identified in this case was the uncontrolled release of 
toluene vapors in an enclosed space with an active ignition source. This hazard 
represented the highest level of risk because it combined a highly flammable 
substance with a non-compliant ignition source and inadequate ventilation, creating 
conditions for a flash fire. 
‚óè‚Äã Probability: High (frequent maintenance near live feed lines, no gas 
monitoring) 
‚óè‚Äã Severity: Critical (potential for fatality, severe burns, and major equipment 
loss) 
‚óè‚Äã Risk Level: Extreme (immediate action required) 
Risk Assessment Process 
Following hazard identification, the investigation team conducted a formal risk 
assessment using the 5x5 Risk Matrix method, which evaluates each hazard based 
on the likelihood of occurrence and the severity of potential consequences. Each 
hazard was assigned a risk score to determine priority for control measures. 
a.‚Äã Toluene vapor accumulation in enclosed area: Likelihood 5 (Almost Certain), 
Severity 5 (Catastrophic) = Risk Score 25 (Extreme) 
b.‚Äã Non-explosion-proof motor in hazardous zone: Likelihood 4 (Likely), Severity 
5 (Catastrophic) = Risk Score 20 (High) 
c.‚Äã Corroded valve on feed line: Likelihood 4 (Likely), Severity 4 (Major) = Risk 
Score 16 (High) 
d.‚Äã Absence of PTW for hot/maintenance work: Likelihood 5 (Almost Certain), 
Severity 4 (Major) = Risk Score 20 (High) 
e.‚Äã Inadequate PPE usage: Likelihood 4 (Likely), Severity 3 (Moderate) = Risk 
Score 12 (Medium)

Methods Used for Hazard Identification 
After the incident, ChemiCore Industries adopted a structured approach to hazard 
identification that integrated both proactive and reactive methods. The objective was 
to prevent recurrence and to develop a comprehensive hazard register for the entire 
plant. 
‚óè‚Äã Walkthrough Inspections: Systematic physical inspections of all reactor bays, 
storage areas, and utility zones conducted by the safety officer and a 
cross-functional team. 
‚óè‚Äã Incident Investigation Analysis: Root cause analysis (RCA) using the 
Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram method was applied to the flash fire incident to 
trace all contributing factors. 
‚óè‚Äã Hazard and Operability Study (HAZOP): A HAZOP study was initiated for all 
major process units to identify deviations from intended design parameters. 
‚óè‚Äã Near-Miss Reporting Review: Historical near-miss records from the past three 
years were reviewed to identify recurring patterns and unreported hazards. 
‚óè‚Äã Checklist-Based Inspection: OSHA-aligned inspection checklists were used 
for evaluating compliance in electrical safety, chemical storage, and PPE 
usage. 
Tools and Techniques Applied 
The safety team deployed a combination of structured tools and field-based 
techniques to ensure comprehensive coverage: 
1.‚Äã Job Safety Analysis (JSA): Each high-risk job was broken into sequential 
steps, and hazards at each step were identified along with recommended 

 
controls. JSA sheets were prepared for maintenance, vessel entry, and 
solvent transfer tasks. 
2.‚Äã LOTO Audit: A full audit of lockout/tagout compliance was conducted across 
all isolation points in the plant. Colour-coded LOTO padlocks and shadow 
boards were introduced. 
3.‚Äã Area Classification Review: All electrical equipment in the plant was 
re-evaluated against ATEX/IECEx zone classification requirements. 
4.‚Äã Toolbox Talks: Daily pre-shift safety briefings (toolbox talks) were formally 
institutionalized for all work crews, covering task-specific hazards. 
 
Role of Employees in Hazard Reporting 
The investigation revealed that several hazards, including the corroded valve and the 
non-compliant motor, had been noticed by workers previously but were not reported 
due to fear of reprisal and a lack of accessible reporting channels. 
‚óã‚Äã An anonymous hazard reporting system (digital and paper-based) was 
introduced post-incident. 
‚óã‚Äã Workers were trained on their legal right and responsibility to report hazards 
under the Factories Act, 1948. 
‚óã‚Äã A 'Safety Observer' programme was launched to recognise and reward 
proactive hazard reporting. 
‚óã‚Äã Monthly safety committee meetings were made mandatory, with worker 
representation from all departments. 
‚óã‚Äã Supervisors received refresher training on creating a psychologically safe 
environment for hazard reporting. 
 
 
 
 

 
Control Measures Implemented 
Based on the hierarchy of controls (Elimination, Substitution, Engineering Controls, 
Administrative Controls, PPE), ChemiCore Industries implemented the following 
measures: 
Elimination and Substitution: 
1.‚Äã Wherever possible, toluene was substituted with a less volatile, water-based 
solvent for certain adhesive formulations. 
Engineering Controls: 
2.‚Äã All electrical equipment in Reactor Bay No. 3 was replaced with 
ATEX-certified, explosion-proof models. 
3.‚Äã A fixed continuous gas detection system for toluene was installed with audible 
and visual alarms. 
4.‚Äã A forced mechanical ventilation system was installed in all reactor bays. 
5.‚Äã All corroded valves on feed lines were replaced with stainless steel ball valves 
rated for the operating pressure. 
Administrative Controls: 
6.‚Äã A formal Permit to Work (PTW) system was introduced and made mandatory 
for all maintenance, hot work, and vessel entry operations. 
7.‚Äã A comprehensive LOTO procedure was documented and all workers were 
trained and certified. 
8.‚Äã Pre-task hazard assessments (JSA) became mandatory before the start of 
each shift. 
PPE Upgrades: 
9.‚Äã Workers were issued chemical-resistant gloves, face shields, and chemical 
splash suits for all solvent handling tasks. 
10.‚ÄãRespiratory protection (half-face respirators with organic vapour cartridges) 
was mandated in all reactor bays. 
Conclusion 
‚ûî‚Äã The ChemiCore Industries flash fire incident of February 2022 serves as a 
powerful reminder that hazard identification must be a continuous, systematic, 
and participatory process rather than a periodic compliance exercise. The 
incident was not caused by a single failure but by a series of interconnected 
organizational, procedural, and physical deficiencies that had developed over 
time. 
‚ûî‚Äã Effective hazard identification, supported by appropriate tools such as JSA, 
HAZOP, and walkthrough inspections, and reinforced by strong safety 
leadership and employee engagement, can prevent such incidents. 
Organizations must embed hazard identification into their daily operational 
culture, ensuring that every worker is empowered to recognize and report 
hazards without fear. 
‚ûî‚Äã The corrective actions implemented at ChemiCore Industries demonstrate 
that even after a serious incident, a structured and committed response can 

 
transform workplace safety outcomes. Continuous monitoring, training, and 
review remain essential to sustaining these improvements.
