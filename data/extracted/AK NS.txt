Abstract 
The WannaCry ransomware attack in May 2017 was a major global cyberattack that 
affected thousands of computers worldwide. It exploited a vulnerability in Microsoft 
Windows called EternalBlue to spread quickly across networks. The attack encrypted 
user data and demanded ransom payment in Bitcoin. Many important organizations, 
including the UK’s National Health Service, were seriously affected. This case study 
briefly explains how the attack occurred, its impact, and the key cybersecurity lessons 
learned. It emphasizes the importance of regular system updates and strong security 
practices to prevent similar attacks. 
Introduction 
Overview of the WannaCry Ransomware Attack 
The WannaCry ransomware attack in May 2017 was a major global cyberattack that 
affected hundreds of thousands of computers across more than 150 countries. It targeted 
Microsoft Windows systems by exploiting the EternalBlue vulnerability, which was 
originally developed by the U.S. National Security Agency and later leaked. WannaCry 
functioned as a cryptoworm, allowing it to spread automatically across networks without 
user interaction. Once infected, systems had their data encrypted and were presented 
with a ransom demand in Bitcoin. Critical organizations, including the UK’s National 
Health Service (NHS), were severely disrupted, highlighting serious weaknesses in 
cybersecurity preparedness worldwide. 
 

 
Importance of Cybersecurity Case Studies 
Cybersecurity case studies are essential for understanding real-world cyber threats and 
their impacts. They help analyze how attacks occur, identify exploited vulnerabilities, and 
evaluate the effectiveness of security measures. The WannaCry attack is an important 
case study because it demonstrates how unpatched systems and poor vulnerability 
management can lead to widespread damage. Studying such incidents improves 
awareness, strengthens defensive strategies, and supports better decision-making in 
cybersecurity planning and risk management. 
Objectives and Scope of the Study 
The objective of this case study is to analyze the WannaCry ransomware attack, focusing 
on its causes, methods of propagation, and consequences. It aims to highlight key 
cybersecurity failures and lessons learned from the incident. The scope of the study is 
limited to the WannaCry attack of May 2017, emphasizing its technical and organizational 
impact rather than legal or forensic aspects. This study is intended to enhance academic 
understanding of ransomware threats and cybersecurity resilience. 
Why Ransomware Is Used in the Current World 
 Ransomware is widely used in the current world because it is a highly profitable 
and low-risk cybercrime for attackers. The rapid growth of digital systems, cloud 
services, and online data storage has increased the number of potential targets, 
including individuals, businesses, hospitals, and government organizations.  
 Many organizations depend heavily on digital data for daily operations, making 
them more likely to pay ransom to quickly restore access. The use of 
cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin enables attackers to receive payments 
anonymously, reducing the chances of being traced.  
 In addition, weak cybersecurity practices, delayed software updates, and the 
continued use of legacy systems increase vulnerability to ransomware attacks. 
The availability of Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) has further simplified the 
process, allowing even non-technical criminals to launch attacks.  
 As a result, ransomware has become one of the most effective and commonly 
used cyber threats in the modern digital world. 
Cybersecurity and Modern Threats 
Cybersecurity is the protection of computers, networks, and data from cyberattacks and 
unauthorized access. It helps to keep information safe from threats such as viruses, 
malware, ransomware, and hacking. Cybersecurity ensures that digital systems work 
safely and reliably. 
 Growth of Digital Infrastructure 

 
Digital infrastructure has grown rapidly due to the use of the internet, cloud

systems for daily operations. As digital usage increases, the need to protect these 
systems also becomes more important.  
 Rise of Global Cyber Attacks  
Global cyberattacks have increased with the growth of digital technology. Attackers 
target organizations, governments, and individuals to steal data or disrupt 
services. Ransomware attacks, such as WannaCry, show how cyber threats can

Timeline of Ransomware Evolution 
1989 – AIDS Trojan: 
The first known ransomware attack, AIDS Trojan, demanded victims to send payment 
through postal mail to regain access to their systems. 
2005–2010 – Early Screen Lockers: 
During this period, ransomware mainly locked user screens and displayed fake security 
or law enforcement warnings to force victims into paying money. 
2013 – CryptoLocker: 
CryptoLocker marked a major shift by using strong encryption and demanding ransom 
payments in Bitcoin, making tracking and recovery difficult. 
2015 – CryptoWall: 
CryptoWall improved encryption techniques and used anonymous payment systems, 
increasing the success rate of ransomware attacks. 
2016 – Locky: 
Locky spread rapidly through malicious email attachments and macros, showing how 
social engineering could enhance ransomware distribution. 
2017 – WannaCry: 
WannaCry used worm-like propagation by exploiting the EternalBlue vulnerability, 
causing a global outbreak and massive disruptions. 
2018–2020 – Ryuk and Maze: 
These ransomware strains specifically targeted large enterprises and organizations, 
demanding higher ransoms and causing operational shutdowns. 
2021–Present – Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS): 
Ransomware became commercialized, with cybercriminals selling ready-made 
ransomware tools to others, increasing attack frequency worldwide.

Understanding Ransomware 
Ransomware is a malicious software designed to block access to computer systems or 
encrypt important files and then demand a ransom payment for their recovery. Attackers 
usually request payment in cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin to remain anonymous. 
Ransomware attacks commonly spread through phishing emails, malicious downloads, 
and network vulnerabilities. 
Over time, ransomware has evolved from simple file-locking malware into a powerful 
cyber threat capable of spreading automatically across networks. Modern ransomware 
can target large organizations, disrupt essential services, and cause significant financial 
and operational damage. In some cases, ransomware is used strategically to attack 
critical infrastructure such as hospitals, transportation systems, and government 
networks. 
 

Background of WannaCry 
Origin and Discovery 
WannaCry ransomware emerged in May 2017 as a new and highly aggressive cyber 
threat targeting Microsoft Windows operating systems. It exploited a vulnerability in the 
Windows Server Message Block (SMB) protocol using an exploit known as EternalBlue. 
This exploit was originally developed by the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) and 
later leaked by a hacker group called the Shadow Brokers. Once the vulnerability became 
public, cybercriminals used it to develop WannaCry, enabling the malware to spread 
rapidly across unpatched systems. The ransomware was first detected when 
organizations began reporting sudden system lockouts and ransom demands. 
Timeline of the Attack (May 2017) 
The WannaCry attack began on May 12, 2017, and spread globally within hours. As the 
ransomware propagated automatically, thousands of systems were infected in a very 
short time. On the same day, security researchers identified a “kill switch” in the malware 
code, which helped slow down its spread. Despite this, the attack continued to affect 
systems over the following days, especially those that had not applied recent security 
updates. Microsoft later released emergency patches to protect unsupported versions of 
Windows, and organizations worldwide began urgent recovery efforts. 
 Initial Affected Regions  
The initial impact of WannaCry was observed across multiple countries simultaneously, 
making it a truly global cyberattack. Major infections were reported in Europe, Asia, and 
North America. The United Kingdom was one of the most severely affected regions, 
particularly within the National Health Service (NHS), where hospital operations were 
disrupted. Other heavily affected countries included Spain, Russia, China, and India. The 
widespread nature of the attack highlighted how interconnected global digital systems 
are and how quickly cyber threats can cross national boundaries. 
 
 

Vulnerability Environment 
The rapid spread of the WannaCry ransomware was largely due to weaknesses in the 
vulnerability environment of affected systems. Many organizations were still using 
outdated Microsoft Windows operating systems that lacked adequate security 
protections. In addition, critical security updates released by Microsoft were not applied 
on time, leaving systems exposed to the EternalBlue exploit. Weak organizational 
security practices, such as poor patch management, limited network monitoring, and lack 
of cybersecurity awareness, further increased the risk of infection. These combined 
vulnerabilities created a favorable environment for WannaCry to spread quickly and 
cause widespread disruption. 
EternalBlue Exploit 
EternalBlue is a cyber exploit that targets a vulnerability in Microsoft Windows operating 
systems. It was originally developed by the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) for 
intelligence purposes. The exploit allows attackers to remotely execute malicious code 
on vulnerable systems without user interaction. When used in attacks like WannaCry, 
EternalBlue enabled rapid and automated infection of unpatched computers.  
 SMB Protocol Vulnerability 
The exploit takes advantage of a weakness in the Server Message Block (SMB) 
protocol, which is used for file sharing and communication between systems in a 
network. A flaw in the SMB version 1 (SMBv1) implementation allowed attackers 
to send specially crafted packets to a target system and gain unauthorized access. 
This vulnerability made it possible for malware to spread quickly across networks. 
 Exploit Leakage and Misuse 
In 2017, EternalBlue was leaked to the public by a hacker group known as the 
Shadow Brokers. Once released, cybercriminals began misusing the exploit to 
launch large-scale cyberattacks. WannaCry was one of the most notable attacks 
to use EternalBlue, demonstrating how leaked cyber weapons can be repurposed 
for criminal activities and cause global disruption. 
Infection Mechanism 
The WannaCry ransomware initially compromised systems by exploiting the EternalBlue 
vulnerability in unpatched Microsoft Windows machines. Once a vulnerable system was 
identified, the malware gained unauthorized access without requiring any user action. 
After entering the system, WannaCry scanned the local network to identify other 
vulnerable computers. It then used automated exploitation techniques to spread rapidly 
across connected systems using the SMB protocol. This self-propagating behavior 
allowed the ransomware to infect thousands of machines within a short period, making 
containment extremely difficult. 
 

Worm-Like Propagation 
 The WannaCry ransomware exhibited worm-like propagation, meaning it could 
spread automatically from one system to another without requiring user interaction 
such as clicking a link or opening an attachment. Once a system was infected, 
WannaCry immediately began searching for other vulnerable computers within the 
same local network and across connected networks. This self-spreading behavior 
significantly increased the speed and reach of the attack. 
 WannaCry performed rapid network scanning by sending specially crafted 
requests through the Windows Server Message Block (SMB) protocol. Systems 
that had not applied the necessary security patches were easily compromised. 
The malware used the EternalBlue exploit to gain access and replicate itself on 
newly discovered vulnerable machines. This automated process allowed the 
ransomware to move laterally within organizations, infecting large numbers of 
computers in a short time. 
 The speed and scale of WannaCry’s spread were unprecedented compared to 
earlier ransomware attacks. Within a few hours, the malware had infected 
hundreds of thousands of systems across multiple countries. Large organizations 
with interconnected networks were particularly affected, as the ransomware could 
quickly move between departments and systems. 
Encryption Process 
 File Encryption Method 
After successfully infecting a system, WannaCry initiated the file encryption 
process using advanced cryptographic techniques. The ransomware selectively 
encrypted commonly used file formats, including documents, images, 
presentations, and databases, while avoiding critical system files to ensure the 
operating system remained functional. This approach allowed the malware to keep 
the system running so that victims could view the ransom message and follow 
payment instructions. The encryption process was fast and difficult to reverse 
without the correct decryption key. 
 
 Locking of User Data 
Once encryption was completed, all targeted files became inaccessible to the user. 
WannaCry modified file extensions and stored encryption information within the 
system, making manual recovery extremely difficult. Users were unable to open, 
edit, or transfer their files, effectively locking all important data. This sudden loss 
of access caused panic among users and disrupted normal activities in both 
personal and organizational environments.  
 
 
 
 

 
 
      Impact on System Usability 
Although the infected system continued to operate at a basic level, its usability 
was severely reduced. Essential data required for daily operations was 
unavailable, forcing organizations to suspend services and workflows. A ransom 
notification appeared on the screen, warning that files would remain permanently 
locked unless payment was made within a limited time. This situation caused 
financial losses, operational delays, and increased pressure on affected 
organizations to respond quickly. 
Ransom Demand Strategy 
Ransom Message Design 
After encrypting user data, WannaCry displayed a ransom message on the infected 
system’s screen. The message clearly informed victims that their files had been 
encrypted and could only be recovered by paying a ransom. It included instructions in 
multiple languages, making it understandable to users across different regions. The 
message also showed a countdown timer, indicating a deadline for payment, which 
increased urgency and fear among victims. 
Bitcoin Payment System 
WannaCry demanded ransom payments exclusively in Bitcoin, a digital cryptocurrency. 
Bitcoin was chosen because it allows attackers to receive payments anonymously and 
across international borders. The ransom amount was fixed and increased if payment 
was not made within the given time. 
 

Psychological Pressure Techniques 
The ransom strategy relied heavily on psychological pressure to force victims into quick 
payment. Threats such as permanent data loss after the deadline created panic and 
stress. The increasing ransom amount and countdown timer added further pressure. In 
critical environments like hospitals and businesses, the fear of service disruption and data 
loss made victims more likely to consider paying the ransom. These techniques were 
designed to exploit emotional responses rather than technical weaknesses.

Kill Switch Discovery 
 Discovery of Kill Switch Domain 
During the rapid spread of WannaCry, security researcher Marcus Hutchins 
discovered a hidden “kill switch” in the malware’s code. The ransomware checked 
for a specific, unregistered domain before proceeding with encryption. Hutchins 
registered this domain, which effectively activated the kill switch and stopped the 
malware from spreading further on connected systems. 
 How It Reduced the Spread 
The activation of the kill switch significantly slowed down WannaCry’s propagation. 
Once the domain was registered, infected systems that checked the domain 
stopped executing the encryption process and halted further network scanning. 
This simple intervention prevented thousands of additional infections and bought 
time for organizations to apply security patches and protect vulnerable systems. 

 
 Limitations of the Kill Switch 
While the kill switch reduced the global impact of the attack, it did not reverse the 
damage already caused. Systems that had been infected before the domain 
registration remained locked, and data encrypted by WannaCry could not be 
recovered without backups or decryption tools. Additionally, modified variants of 
the ransomware were later observed that bypassed the kill switch, demonstrating 
that relying solely on such mechanisms is not a comprehensive cybersecurity 
solution. 
Threat Landscape Before the WannaCry Attack 
Prior to the WannaCry ransomware attack in May 2017, the global cyber threat 
environment was already showing clear signs of escalation. Organizations worldwide 
were increasingly dependent on digital systems, cloud services, and networked 
infrastructures to support daily operations. However, cybersecurity readiness did not 
keep pace with this rapid technological expansion. Many institutions continued to operate 
legacy systems that were no longer supported or regularly updated, creating widespread 
security gaps. 
Ransomware attacks existed before WannaCry, but they were generally limited in scope 
and relied heavily on social engineering techniques such as phishing emails or malicious 
attachments. Large-scale, self-propagating ransomware that could spread automatically 
across networks was not widely anticipated. Security patch management was often 
delayed due to operational concerns, lack of awareness, or insufficient IT resources. 
Additionally, employee understanding of cyber risks remained low, increasing exposure 
to malware infections. This combination of outdated systems, weak preventive controls, 
and limited preparedness created a vulnerable digital environment, allowing WannaCry 
to spread rapidly and cause unprecedented global disruption.

Attack Timeline Deep Dive (WannaCry 2017) 
Initial Outbreak and Rapid Escalation 
The WannaCry ransomware attack began on 12 May 2017, when systems across 
multiple countries were suddenly infected within hours. The malware exploited a  

 
vulnerability in the Windows SMB protocol using the EternalBlue exploit, allowing it to 
spread automatically without user interaction. Organizations running unpatched or 
outdated Windows systems were immediately compromised, leading to rapid escalation 
across networks and geographic regions. 
Peak Infection Period 
Within the first 24 hours, WannaCry reached its peak infection stage, affecting 
hundreds of thousands of computers in over 150 countries. Critical sectors such as 
healthcare, transportation, banking, and government services experienced severe 
disruptions. During this phase, systems were encrypted at scale, ransom messages 
were displayed, and operational paralysis became widespread, especially in institutions 
like the UK’s NHS. 
Containment and Decline Phase 
The spread of WannaCry began to slow after cybersecurity researchers discovered a 
built-in kill switch domain within the malware. Registering this domain significantly 
reduced further propagation of the ransomware. Following this, organizations 
implemented emergency patching, network isolation, and system shutdowns to contain 
the threat. Over time, coordinated global response efforts led to the gradual decline of 
the attack, though recovery and system restoration continued for weeks after the initial 
outbreak. 
Technical Evolution of Ransomware Leading to WannaCry 
From a technical perspective, ransomware evolved significantly before the WannaCry 
attack in 2017. Early ransomware relied on basic system lock mechanisms and simple 
scripts, which were relatively easy to detect and remove.  
As defensive technologies improved, attackers adopted stronger cryptographic 
techniques such as symmetric and asymmetric encryption to ensure that victim data 
could not be recovered without a decryption key. 
 The integration of public-key cryptography allowed attackers to securely control 
decryption keys from remote servers. 
A major technical shift occurred when ransomware began exploiting network-level 
vulnerabilities instead of relying on user actions. WannaCry represented a critical 
milestone by combining ransomware payloads with worm-like propagation capabilities. 
 It exploited the Server Message Block (SMBv1) protocol vulnerability using the 
EternalBlue exploit, enabling remote code execution without user interaction. Once a 
vulnerable system was compromised, WannaCry automatically scanned local and  
 

 
external networks to identify additional targets. This automated exploitation allowed the 
malware to spread rapidly across large networks. 
The attack also demonstrated the increasing modularity of ransomware, where 
scanning, exploitation, encryption, and command-and-control communication were 
integrated into a single automated framework.  
This technical sophistication marked a transition from opportunistic attacks to large-
scale, self-propagating cyber threats, highlighting the need for advanced intrusion 
detection, vulnerability management, and network segmentation.

Importance of Cyber Resilience in Modern Organizations 
Cyber resilience represents an organization’s capability to anticipate, withstand, recover 
from, and adapt to cyber incidents while ensuring continuity of critical operations. The 
WannaCry ransomware attack clearly demonstrated that traditional security controls 
alone are insufficient to protect organizations from large-scale cyber threats.  
Many affected institutions lacked structured incident response plans, reliable backup 
systems, and tested disaster recovery mechanisms, which significantly prolonged 
service outages and operational disruption.  
Cyber resilience focuses on proactive preparation, including regular data backups, 
network redundancy, employee training, and well-defined recovery procedures. 
 It also emphasizes rapid detection, containment, and coordinated response to minimize 
damage during an attack. By integrating cyber resilience into organizational strategy, 
institutions can reduce downtime, limit financial and reputational losses, and maintain 
essential services even during severe cyber incidents.  
The lessons from WannaCry highlight cyber resilience as a critical requirement for long-
term digital sustainability and operational stability in the modern threat landscape.

Cyber Security Awareness and Training 
Cyber security awareness and training focus on educating users about cyber threats 
and safe digital practices. Many cyber incidents occur due to human mistakes rather 
than technical failures. Proper training helps employees identify threats early and 
respond correctly, reducing the overall risk of cyberattacks. 
Key Points 
 Awareness of Cyber Threats: 
Employees learn about common threats such as phishing, ransomware, and 
malware, helping them recognize attacks before damage occurs. 
 Safe Online Practices: 
Training promotes secure behavior, including avoiding suspicious links, using 
trusted websites, and following security guidelines. 
 Password and Access Management: 
Users are taught to create strong passwords, avoid password sharing, and use 
multi-factor authentication where available. 
 Importance of Software Updates: 
Awareness programs highlight the need for timely system and application 
updates to fix security vulnerabilities. 
 Incident Reporting: 
Employees are encouraged to report suspicious activities immediately, enabling 
faster response and containment. 
 

 
 Building a Security Culture: 
Regular training creates a sense of responsibility and shared accountability for 
cybersecurity across the organization. 
Ransomware Attribution and Challenges 
Attributing ransomware attacks to specific actors is a major challenge due to the 
sophisticated techniques used by cybercriminals. Attackers often hide their identities 
using anonymization tools such as Tor networks, VPNs, and encrypted communication 
channels. They also demand payments in cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin, which are 
difficult to trace.  
In the WannaCry attack, investigations suggested links to state-sponsored groups, but 
concrete legal attribution was complicated by the malware’s global spread and the 
reuse of existing exploits like EternalBlue.  
Attackers can also reuse malware code, deploy false flags, and leverage Ransomware-
as-a-Service platforms, making it hard to identify the original perpetrator. These 
challenges complicate law enforcement, delay international cooperation, and limit 
accountability.  
To improve attribution, organizations and governments need advanced cyber forensics, 
coordinated intelligence sharing, and standardized global reporting frameworks. The 
WannaCry case clearly illustrated that without proper attribution mechanisms, holding 
attackers responsible and preventing future attacks becomes extremely difficult.

Conclusion  
 The WannaCry ransomware attack of May 2017 stands as a landmark event in 
the history of global cybersecurity. By exploiting unpatched Windows systems 
through the EternalBlue vulnerability, the attack demonstrated how a single 
weakness could trigger widespread disruption across critical sectors such as 
healthcare, transportation, banking, and government services. The incident 
exposed serious gaps in patch management, legacy system maintenance, 
incident response readiness, and communication strategies. It also highlighted 
the real-world consequences of cyber threats, including economic loss, service 
disruption, and risks to public safety. 
 
 Overall, the WannaCry case study emphasizes that cybersecurity is no longer a 
purely technical issue but a strategic and organizational responsibility. The attack 
reshaped global cybersecurity awareness, leading to stronger policies, improved 
ransomware defenses, and increased collaboration between governments, 
technology vendors, and the cybersecurity community. As future threats become 
more advanced with AI-driven and autonomous attacks, the lessons learned from 
WannaCry underline the need for proactive defense, continuous monitoring, 
regular updates, and cyber resilience planning to protect digital infrastructure and 
society at large.
